Dr. Tony Jurich (July 9, 1947 to October 13, 2010)

The Jurich Affirmative Mental Health Conference honors Dr. Tony Jurich, a professor in the couple and family therapy program at Kansas State University. He began his career at K-State in 1972 and led by example in the couple and family program, where he taught for more than 39 years before his demise in 2010.

Tony was loved and respected as a dedicated therapist, an enthusiastic teacher and researcher, and a wise supervisor and mentor. He earned national recognition for his work as a leader in the field of family therapy.

Tony's life and work embodied the core principles of affirmative therapy, and he challenged his students and colleagues to do the same. Tony's core leadership tenets strive for resonance; assume value and be respectful; be compassionate; do what is right; affirm everyone; and lead by example - provide a roadmap for sustainable community change.

In 2014, Tony's wife, Olivia Collins, and his brother, Steve Jurich, established the Tony Jurich Community Commitment and Leadership Student Awards, which recognize graduating students who demonstrate a commitment to these leadership tenets.

The couple and family therapy program is housed in the Department of Applied Human Sciences, College of Health and Human Sciences. The program's goal is to develop distinguished scholars who embrace affirmative care and who are highly sought after because they demonstrate competency as world-class researchers, excellent clinicians, clinical supervisors and educators.

Mental health providers are at especially high risk for burnout and compassion fatigue. While they report being wholly committed to (and loving) their work, they are oftentimes overwhelmed with the intensity of their caseloads, workplace and interdisciplinary politics, administrative demands, and tendencies to underreport personal struggles or seek help. In this workshop, empirically supported understandings of this problem, alongside strategies across provider-, team-, and care systems- levels to prevent and/or mitigate it, will be shared.

9:00 – 11:00 am *Plenary
In this presentation, we will discuss how to be an encompassing clinician for clients who do not identify as White. We will take a deep dive into what it means to be an encompassing therapist and how to represent yourself and educate yourself on the realities these communities are facing currently and historically, to ensure that your practice, whether individual or group, decenters ideologies and norms that are exclusionary. We will also discuss special and ethical considerations when it comes to diagnosing these populations to ensure acceptance and safety.

Dr. Lorin Kelly (She/her) is a fully licensed therapist in both Nebraska and Kansas and an approved supervisor from the American Association of Marriage and Family Therapists. She is the owner of Kelly and Associates Therapeutic Services, a group practice situated in Lincoln, NE. The practice specializes in serving BIPOC (Black, Indigenous, and People of Color) populations, LGBTQ+ folks, and those dealing with complex trauma. Dr. Kelly is a certified EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) therapist and is actively working towards becoming an EMDR consultant. Furthermore, she has been a two-time award winner of the AAMFT Minority Fellowship program and won the 2021 AAMFT Emerging Leaders award while completing her PhD at Kansas State University. She is passionate about the training of inclusive and trauma-informed clinicians and is a practicum site for developing therapists to aid in their development to ensure inclusive and competent mental health care for our community.

11:15 am – 12:15 pm Breakout Session
*
LGBTQ+ people experience significant rates of depression, anxiety, and trauma due to discrimination rooted in cissexism and heterosexism. In the current US socio-political climate, religious-based discourse and legislative decisions that are biased, discriminatory, and hateful towards LGBTQ+ people are especially prevalent. This presentation will focus on specific strategies and interventions that therapists can use to support LGBTQ+ people from religious backgrounds who have experienced religious-based non-affirmation of their identities and religious trauma. Considerations will include LGBTQ+ people who are actively involved in religious communities as well as those who have disaffiliated by choice or who have been rejected from religious communities. An intentional focus will be supporting therapists in providing autonomic nervous-system informed care with the understanding that religious trauma experienced by LGBTQ+ people occurs within interpersonal, family, and socio-political contexts. Relevant ethical and diagnosis issues will also be addressed.
Katelyn Coburn, Assistant Professor, University of Nebraska-Lincoln
